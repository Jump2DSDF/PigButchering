## Step 1: Initial Contact Analysis (접근 및 유인)

### Reasoning:
The narrative explicitly states "The resident learned about TSAI through WhatsApp." This provides clear evidence of the initial contact platform.

**Initial Contact Platform:** WhatsApp is explicitly mentioned as the discovery/contact channel. This is a common platform for Pig Butchering scams as it allows for:
- International communication with minimal friction
- End-to-end encryption (perceived privacy)
- Easy sharing of multimedia (fake trading screenshots)
- Lower scrutiny compared to traditional financial channels

**Lure Type:** The narrative describes this as a "crypto asset exchange" that the victim "learned about" through WhatsApp. This suggests:
- Primary lure: Investment opportunity (crypto exchange platform)
- The scammer likely presented TSAI as a legitimate trading platform with attractive features
- No explicit mention of romance, friendship, or other relationship-building tactics
- Direct focus on investment/trading opportunity

**Communication Migration:** The narrative does not indicate any migration to other platforms. The victim appears to have:
1. Learned about TSAI via WhatsApp
2. Directly opened an account on the fraudulent exchange website
3. Conducted transactions through the platform itself

The communication likely remained on WhatsApp for guidance/support while the actual trading interface was the website.

**Confidence:** High (0.85) - WhatsApp contact is explicitly stated; investment lure is strongly implied by context.

## Step 3: Fraud Mechanism Analysis (사기 수법)

### Reasoning:
This section has the clearest evidence in the narrative.

**Platform Type:** Explicitly described as "crypto asset exchange"
- Fake exchange platform (not wallet, not trading platform in traditional sense)
- Designed to mimic legitimate cryptocurrency exchanges
- Classification: **fake_exchange**

**Platform Names:**
- Primary: "TSAI Exchange" (explicitly named)
- "TSAI" appears to be a branded name for the fraudulent operation

**Platform URLs:** Two URLs provided:
1. "http://www.techstarvoip.com/" (main site)
2. "https://office.techstarvoip.com/" (likely customer portal/trading interface)

Note: The domain "techstarvoip.com" is incongruous - "voip" typically refers to Voice over IP telecommunications, not cryptocurrency exchanges. This mismatch is a red flag indicating:
- Domain repurposing or typosquatting
- Deliberate obfuscation
- Technical sophistication in creating believable subdomains

**Fake Profits Shown:** Not explicitly stated in the narrative, but:
- Typical exchange platforms display account balances
- The victim's inability to "recover their funds" implies they could see funds in the account
- Standard practice in Pig Butchering: show growing balance to encourage more deposits
- Assessment: **Likely yes** (high probability but not explicitly confirmed)

**Withdrawal Block Tactics:** The narrative states "The resident has been unable to recover their funds from the exchange."

Possible blocking mechanisms (typical for exchange scams):
- Account verification requirements
- Withdrawal fees or minimum balance requirements
- "Security holds" or "risk management" freezes
- Trading volume requirements before withdrawal
- Tax payment demands
- KYC/AML compliance demands
- Platform "technical issues"
- Eventually: complete shutdown (site no longer active)

**Final Exit:** "The website is no longer active" - classic exit scam pattern. The operators disappeared with all funds.

**Confidence:** High (0.90) - Platform type and shutdown are explicit; withdrawal blocking strongly implied.
